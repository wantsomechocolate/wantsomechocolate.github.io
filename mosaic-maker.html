Mosaic Maker

Examples and Explanations

Some Helpful Vocabulary

The Target Image is broken up into Sections. Images are chosen from a library which I call Pieces in order to create a Photomosaic. Pieces are assigned to sections using a Comparison Algorithm which is typically comprised of a Reducing Function and an Error Function.

The Goal

Programmatically create fidelitous and aesthetic photomosaics while minimizing the number of sections and maximizing the number of unique pieces without the use of semi-transparant overlays

A Brief History of Climb

This example walks through some of the major parameters for mosaic building and how changing them affects the result. It also serves as a sort of brief history of the project as most of these parameters were considered/developed in the order presented.

Granularity: A Trade-Off between Fidelity and Ethos

Granularity refers to the number of sections. I think it is generally considered more impressive to use fewer sections when building a mosaic. The point of a photomosaic is to let the detail in each small image combine to produce the target. That being said, more sections leads to better fidelity, which is often hard to resist.
I found that between 30 and 50 sections across the shorter dimension is a good balance point. But it really depends on many factors, such as the ratio between the physical dimensions of the final product and the distance from which it will be viewed, as well as the amount of detail in the target-image.

Piece Library: The More Comprehensive The Better

More (and more varied) pieces yield better fidelity, especially color fidelity. It is important to groom the piece library, though. For example, pictures with a border or with words on them typically don't make for good pieces. I also avoid images that are close variants of each other. Somewhere in the neighborhood of 1000 pieces is a good start.
My goal for this mosaic was to use my own vacation photos as the pieces. After curation I wound up with about 500. That's not that many for a piece library so let's see what we can do.

Image Comparison: Reduce Reuse Recycle

A logical first pass when programatically constructing a photomosaic is to reduce sections and pieces down to a single pixel of information that represents them somehow. This works, but is improved by reducing image data slightly less severly, to a 5x5 grid of pixels, for example. I call this comparison-granularity. Finer comparison granularity generally increases fidelity, just look at that armpit in the photo on the right!
Of course, resizing both images to the size of the smaller image and comparing pixel by pixel is also possible, but that takes quite a bit more time and doesn't actually improve fidelity in my experience over using reduced image data because of limitations typical in most piece libraries.

Randomization: And Some Retrospection

So far I've been trying to increase the fidelity, and I think the image on the left (below) isn't too shabby for a photomosaic of a random vacation photo made from other photos of the same vacation (as far as detail goes), but it doesn't look much like a photomosaic with large swaths of the target-image using the same piece over and over. A straightforward approach to alleviating this issue is to introduce a degree of randomization. Fidelity decreases, but more unique pieces are used. Sadly, the armpit photo has changed. I could run the algorthm without randimization, pin pieces I really like, and run again with randomization, but let's take a look at another method.

Neighborhood Constraint: This Town Ain't Big Enough For the Two of Us

Another method to alleviate large areas of the photomosaic choosing the same piece without introducing randomization is to force duplicate pieces to be a certain distance from eachother in the final result. This gives some fidelity back while still increasing the number of unique pieces, however, a repeated pattern now emerges as the best matches are cycled through in a way that satisfies the new contstraint.
I typically use 5 as the number of sections that must separate duplicate pieces.

Piece Count: A Piece Has Got To Know Their Limitations

To help with this repeated pattern, one option is to enforce a maximum number of times a piece can be chosen as the best match for a section. But if you set the limit too low relative to the number of pieces in your library, you'll notice that the fidelity plumets as the algorthm starts to run out of pieces to choose from.
There isn't a rule of thumb I found for this one, it really depends on the number of sections relative to the number of pieces, and whether or not you want to maximize unique pieces. For the image on the right, I set max instances to 5 and because there are 2500 sections and only about 500 pieces, the result is that almost every piece will be used 5 times. You'll notice that the top of the mosaic looks similar, but at the bottom the algorithm is basically just choosing pieces that are leftover.

Section Priority: All Sections are Not Created Equal

Setting the section priority is a good way to try and make sure sections that need more detail are handled first while more pieces are available. Before I thought to set section priority, the sections were being populated with pieces from top to bottom and left to right. Now the default is radial (highest in the center and decreasing towards the edges). Other methods of setting section priority include edge detection, face dectection, or manual selection.
