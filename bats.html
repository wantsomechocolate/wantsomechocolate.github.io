Examples and Explanations

The Goal

Create fidelitous and aesthetic photomosaics programmatically without the use of semi-transparant overlays

Some Helpful Vocabulary

The Target Image is broken up into Sections. Images are chosen from a library which I call Pieces in order to create a Photomosaic. Pieces are assigned to sections using a Comparison Algorithm which is typically comprised of a Reducing Function and an Error Function.

Granularity: A Trade-Off between Fidelity and Spirit

Granularity refers to the number of sections. Higher granularity generally yields better fidelity. But go overboard and you're just approximating pixels. I found that between 30 and 50 sections across the shorter dimension is a good balance point.

Piece Library: The More Comprehensive The Better

More (and more varied) pieces generally yields better fidelity. It is important to groom the piece library, though. For example, pictures with a border or with words on them typically don't make for good pieces. I also generally avoid images that are generally the same (when getting images from the internet you'll wind up with a lot of variants of the same image). I also found pictures of eyes don't generally make for great pieces. Somewhere in the neighborhood of 1000 pieces is usually a good start.
The images below use completely different piece libraries. On the left, just 100 images from random google image searches. On the right uses 500 or so images from an extended vacation I took in 2019.

Image Comparison: Reduce Reuse Recycle

A logical first pass when programatically constructing a photomosaic is to reduce sections and pieces down to a single pixel of information that represents the image somehow. This works, but is improved by reducing image data slightly less severly, to say a 5x5 grid of pixels. Just look at that armpit in the photo on the right!
Of course, resizing both images to the size of the smaller image and comparing pixel by pixel is also possible, but that takes quite a bit more time and doesn't actually improve fidelity in my experience over using reduced image data because of limitations typical in most piece libraries.

Randomization: And Some Retrospection

So far I've been trying to increase the fidelity, and I think the image on the left (below) isn't too shabby for a photomosaic of a random vacation photo made from other photos of the same vacation (as far as detail goes), but it doesn't look much like a photomosaic with large swaths of the target-image using the same piece over and over. A straightforward approach to alleviating this issue is to introduce a degree of randomization. Fidelity decreases, but more unique pieces are used, which is in the spirit of creating a photomosaic. Sadly, the armpit photo has changed. I could run the algorthm, pin pieces I really like, and run again, but let's take a look at another method.

Neighborhood Constraint: This Town Ain't Big Enough For the Two of Us

Another method to alleviate large areas of the photomosaic choosing the same piece without introducing randomization is to force duplicate pieces to be a certain distance from eachother in the final result. This gives some fidelity back while still increasing the number of unique pieces, however, a repeated pattern now emerges as the best matches are cycled through in a way that satisfies the new contstraint.

Piece Count: A Piece Has Got To Know Their Limitations

To help with this repeated pattern, one option is to enforce a maximum number of times a piece can be chosen as the best match for a section. But if you set the limit too low relative to the number of pieces in your library, you'll notice that the fidelity plumets as the algorthm starts to run out of pieces to choose from. There isn't a rule of thumb I found for this one, it really depends on the number of sections relative to the number of pieces, and whether or not you want to maximize unique pieces. For the image on the right, I set max instances to 5 and because there are 2500 sections and only about 500 pieces, the result is that almost every piece will be used 5 times. You'll notice that the top of the mosaic looks similar, but at the bottom, the algorithm is basically just choosing pieces that are leftover, we should save the best pieces for where we needed them the most!

Section Priority: All Sections are Not Created Equal

Setting the section priority is a good way to try and make sure sections that need more detail are handled first while more pieces are available. Before I thought to set section priority, the sections were being populated with pieces from top to bottom and left to right. Now the default is radial (highest in the center and decreasing towards the edges). Other methods of setting section priority include edge detection, face dectection, or manual selection
